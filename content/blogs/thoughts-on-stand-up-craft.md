The hardest part about stand-up isn't writing jokes — it's learning to trust that the audience wants you to succeed. Most people come to comedy shows hoping to laugh, not waiting for you to fail. Once you internalize that, everything else gets easier.

Timing isn't just about pauses, though pauses are important. It's about understanding the rhythm of how people process information. You can't rush someone to the punchline, but you also can't let them get there before you do.

I used to think "finding your voice" was about developing a persona or a character. Now I think it's about learning to be the most interesting version of yourself on stage. The audience can tell when you're being authentic, even if your authentic self is weird or anxious or obsessed with mundane details.

The best advice I ever got was to record every set, even the bad ones. Especially the bad ones. You learn more from listening to what didn't work than from basking in what did. Plus, sometimes you'll discover that a bit you thought bombed actually got laughs you didn't notice in the moment.

Stand-up is the only art form where the audience tells you immediately whether you're succeeding. That's terrifying and wonderful in equal measure.
