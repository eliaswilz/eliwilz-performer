My joke-writing process is embarrassingly scattered. I have voice memos on my phone from 2 AM grocery store trips, notebook margins filled with half-thoughts, and a Notes app that's become a graveyard of premises that seemed hilarious at the time.

But there's a pattern to the chaos. The best material usually comes from moments of genuine frustration or confusion. When I'm genuinely annoyed about something mundane — like why coffee shops play music so loud you can't think — that's when the comedy brain kicks in.

I've learned to trust the weird tangents. Sometimes I'll be working on a bit about dating apps and end up with five minutes about why we still say "hang up" the phone. Those unexpected connections often become the strongest parts of a set.

The real work happens in the editing. First drafts are usually too long and too inside-my-own-head. The magic is in finding the universal truth buried in the personal experience.
