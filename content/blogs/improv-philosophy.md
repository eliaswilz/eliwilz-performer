Improv taught me more about comedy than any stand-up class ever could. Not because the skills directly translate — they don't, really — but because it forces you to trust your instincts in real time.

The "yes, and" principle gets talked about to death, but what's more interesting to me is learning when to break it. The best improvisers know when to challenge an offer, when to change direction, when to let a scene die. It's not about following rules; it's about serving the moment.

Being part of Atlas Improv Company has shown me how comedy can be genuinely collaborative. In stand-up, you're alone with your thoughts and the audience. In improv, you're building something together that none of you could have created alone.

The biggest lesson: comedy works best when everyone involved — performers and audience — feels safe to be surprised. That's true whether you're on stage at Atlas or trying out new material at an open mic.
