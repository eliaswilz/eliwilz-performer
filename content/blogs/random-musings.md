Why do we still say "roll down the window" when car windows haven't had actual rolls in decades? And why does everyone understand exactly what you mean when you say it?

I've been thinking about how comedy ages. Jokes that killed five years ago sometimes feel completely different now, not because they're offensive, but because the shared cultural references have shifted. It's like watching a movie from your childhood and realizing half the jokes went over your head the first time.

There's something beautiful about the way laughter works in a room. It's contagious in the most literal sense — people laugh harder when they hear other people laughing. But it's also fragile. One person checking their phone can change the entire energy.

I love that moment in a set when you can feel the audience decide to trust you. It usually happens after the first real laugh, when they realize you're not going to waste their time. From that point on, they're willing to follow you into weirder territory.

The best compliment I ever got after a show was someone saying, "I forgot I was watching comedy." Not because it wasn't funny, but because it felt like a conversation.
